Sir William and a companion stand on the rim of the crater of Monte Barbaro (also called Monte Gauro). Down and to the left is the barren cone of Monte Nuovo. The landscape is pockmarked with ancient volcanoes. Just over the shoreline looms the Mountain of Cuma with its gated entrance (numbers 6 and 7 on plate), where Aeneas sought the aid of the sybil upon first arriving on Italian ground (Virgil, Aeneid, Book VI). The three visible lakes are all of volcanic origin. The largest, Avernus (number 4 on plate), with its sulphurous vapors, became the legendary entrance to the underworld. Of Aeneas’ descent to that realm to visit the shade of his father, Anchises, Virgil wrote:


Campi Phlegraei, plate 28: View from Monte Barbaro.

 

And there th’unnavigable lake extends,
O’er whose unhappy waters, void of light,
No bird presumes to steer his airy flight;
Such deadly stenches from the depths arise,
And steaming sulphur, that infects the skies.
From hence the Grecian bards their legends make,
And give the name Avernus to the lake.
Virgil, Aeneid, Book VI, lines 236-42
(Translated by John Dryden)
